## Revelation

Between the last word of Malachi and the first word of Matthew, there are four hundred years of silence. No prophet spoke. No new Scripture was written. The last thing God said through Malachi was: I will send the prophet Elijah before the great and dreadful day of the Lord comes. And then — nothing. Four hundred years of nothing. And yet in those four hundred years, there were people who did not stop waiting. People who did not interpret the silence as abandonment. People who kept the faith with extraordinary faithfulness through generations that never saw the fulfilment of what they were waiting for.

Simeon is one of the most moving figures in the entire New Testament. Luke tells us he was righteous and devout — these are not casual adjectives. They describe the shape of a life, the accumulated character of decades of faithful living. He was waiting for the consolation of Israel. That waiting was not passive. It was not the waiting of someone parked in neutral, not really living, just holding on until the thing they are waiting for arrives. Simeon's waiting was active — expressed in righteousness, in devotion, in a lifestyle shaped by the promise he was holding. He had been told he would not die before he saw the Messiah, and rather than that promise making him passive, it made him more alive, more attentive, more shaped by holy expectation.

Anna had been in the temple worshipping night and day for decades. She was a widow of eighty-four years who had outlasted her grief and transformed it into intercession. These two people — an old man and an elderly widow — represent the faithful remnant of Israel. The anawim. The poor in spirit who had nothing left to hold onto except the promise of God. And they were the ones who were present when the promise walked through the door. Their waiting had not been wasted. Every decade of faithfulness, every night of prayer, every day of righteous living had been preparation for the moment when they would hold the consolation of Israel in their arms. Waiting well is not time wasted. It is time invested in becoming the person who is ready to receive what is coming.

## Morning Prayer

Father, I sit today with Simeon and Anna — two people who waited a very long time for something they had been promised, and who waited well. I want to be that kind of person. Not someone who waits with impatience and resentment, measuring the distance between the promise and its fulfilment with increasing frustration. Someone who waits with righteousness and devotion, whose daily faithfulness is itself a form of worship, whose whole life is shaped by the confidence that what You have promised will arrive.

I confess that I find waiting difficult. I am more comfortable with resolution than with process, more comfortable with answers than with the long faithfulness of living in the question. Teach me the patience of Simeon. Teach me the devotion of Anna. Let the waiting season I am in — whatever it is, wherever I am between promise and fulfilment — be a season of becoming rather than merely enduring.

And let my waiting be shaped by holy living. The Messiah is coming — He came, and He is coming again. Let the way I live this week be the way someone lives who genuinely believes that. Not someone performing religion, but someone shaped from the inside out by the expectation of His return.

## Daily Practices

- Read Luke 2:22–38 in full — both Simeon's song and Anna's response. Sit with the quality of their encounter with the infant Jesus. These were people whose long faithfulness had made them exquisitely sensitive to the presence of God. Ask: what is my waiting producing in me — bitterness or sensitivity?
- Read Lamentations 3:25–26 — "The Lord is good to those whose hope is in him, to the one who seeks him; it is good to wait quietly for the salvation of the Lord." Meditate on the phrase "wait quietly." What would quiet, faithful, undramatic waiting look like in your specific situation today?
- Journal: "What am I currently waiting for that God has promised? How am I waiting — actively or passively, hopefully or resentfully, in righteousness or in spiritual drift?" Let the answer be honest before it is aspirational.
- Read Isaiah 40:28–31 as your closing meditation — the classic passage on waiting for the Lord and the renewal of strength. Let "those who wait upon the Lord shall renew their strength" be your declaration for whatever waiting season you are currently in.
