## Revelation

The last prayer in the Bible is two words. Come, Lord Jesus. Maranatha — an Aramaic word so early that it was preserved untranslated even in the Greek-speaking church, which tells us something about how deep in the bone of the earliest Christians this longing was. They did not say it as a formula. They said it as people who had seen the risen Christ, who had received the Holy Spirit, who were living with one eye always on the horizon. Come. We are waiting. We are ready. Come.

The whole of this week has been building toward this. Hezekiah trusted God in crisis because he knew that the God who made heaven and earth had not abandoned His people. Ruth and Boaz lived in faithful, ordinary, costly hesed because that is what it looks like when people who know God let that knowledge shape their daily behaviour. Daniel prayed three times a day, oriented toward Jerusalem, because prayer was the expression of a life pointed toward God. Simeon and Anna waited with righteousness and devotion because the quality of your waiting is inseparable from the quality of your character. And now the week ends with 1 John 3:3, which ties all of it together in one sentence: all who have this hope in him purify themselves, just as he is pure.

The hope of Christ's return is not an escape from the responsibility of holy living. It is the most powerful motivation for it. 2 Peter 3:11 asks the most searching question: since everything will be destroyed, what kind of people ought you to be? The answer is not "it doesn't matter so enjoy yourself." The answer is: live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God. The imminent return of Christ does not make your daily faithfulness irrelevant — it makes it urgently, cosmically significant. You are not just living your life. You are living a witness. You are living a testimony. You are living a prayer — the prayer that says with every faithful choice, every act of hesed, every hour of disciplined prayer, every day of righteous waiting: Come, Lord Jesus. Come.

## Morning Prayer

Lord Jesus, I close this week with the oldest prayer of the church on my lips and in my heart: come. Come into the situations I have spread before You like Hezekiah spread his letter. Come into the ordinary faithfulness I am trying to practise like Ruth in the field. Come into the prayer life I am building like Daniel at his window. Come into the waiting I am learning to do like Simeon in the temple. Come.

I live between the already and the not yet — and I want to live that space well. You have already come — the incarnation, the cross, the resurrection, the ascension, the gift of the Holy Spirit. And You are coming again. In between, I have been given the extraordinary privilege of living in such a way that my life points to both. Every act of trust says: He is faithful. Every act of hesed says: His love is real. Every disciplined prayer says: He hears. Every day of holy waiting says: He is worth it.

Let this week have produced something in me that persists beyond Sunday. Not a spiritual high that fades by Tuesday but a deeper, quieter, more durable orientation toward You. I am Yours. I am watching. I am waiting. Come, Lord Jesus. Come.

## Daily Practices

- Read Revelation 22:12–21 in full — the final words of Scripture. Note how many times the theme of coming appears, and how the response of the church to every promise of Christ's return is the same prayer: come. Let those last verses of the Bible be your prayer for the week ahead.
- Review your journal from the whole week. Write one sentence that captures the central thing God said to you across all seven days. This is your testimony from this week. Keep it somewhere you will see it.
- Identify how the four themes connected in your own life this week: where did you need Hezekiah's trust? Where did you see Ruth's providence? Where did you practise Daniel's prayer? Where did you identify with Simeon's waiting? Write it honestly.
- Share something from this week with someone close to you. The word of your testimony is a weapon. The themes of this week — trust, providence, prayer, holy waiting — are things people around you need to hear. Be generous with what God has given you.
