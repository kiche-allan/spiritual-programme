There is a kind of silence that is harder to navigate than any storm. It is not the silence of an empty room — it is the silence of a room where you know someone is present but they are not speaking. You have prayed. You have waited. You have read the Word and listened and returned again and again. And the heavens feel like brass.

If you have been there — or if you are there right now — you are in better company than you might think. The Psalms are full of it. Elijah sat under a broom tree and asked to die. John the Baptist, from prison, sent messengers to ask Jesus: are you really the one, or should we look for someone else? Even the greatest figures in the history of faith have sat in the silence and wondered.

## The Silence Is Not Absence

The first thing that needs to be said is this: the silence of God is not the absence of God. These are two entirely different things, and confusing them is one of the most common sources of unnecessary spiritual suffering.

When Elijah sat under that broom tree, God did not speak to him immediately. But God sent an angel. The angel touched him, gave him food, let him sleep, and then came back a second time. God's response to Elijah's despair was not a sermon — it was bread and rest. Before the still small voice, there was practical, embodied care. The silence of words was filled with the presence of provision.

Psalm 139 says: where can I go from your Spirit? Where can I flee from your presence? If I go up to the heavens, you are there. If I make my bed in the depths, you are there. The omnipresence of God is not a theological abstraction — it is a promise to the person sitting in the silence. You are not sitting there alone. The silence is not empty.

## What the Silence Is Doing

The second thing that needs to be said is harder, and more important: the silence is doing something.

Formation almost never happens in the loud, dramatic, unmistakable moments. It happens in the long, quiet, uncomfortable stretches where you have nothing to hold onto except the character of a God you cannot currently feel. Those stretches are where the roots go down. A tree that has always been watered abundantly does not develop deep roots — it has no need. It is the tree that has had to search for water that develops the root system that will sustain it through the drought.

The silence is not punishment. It is not evidence that you have done something wrong or that God is displeased. In many cases, it is evidence that He trusts you with a deeper work than the one the noise was producing. Simeon waited decades for the consolation of Israel. Anna fasted and prayed for years in the temple. When the moment finally came, they were the only ones in the room who recognised it — because the silence had made them exquisitely attentive to the presence of God.

## How to Wait Well

Waiting in silence is a skill, and like all skills it can be practised well or poorly.

The poor version of waiting is passive — sitting still, doing nothing, allowing the silence to produce bitterness or drift. This is the waiting that ends in either cynicism ("God never speaks anyway") or spiritual drift ("I'll just stop paying attention until He shows up").

The good version of waiting is active. It looks like Daniel — who prayed three times a day toward Jerusalem not because it felt good but because it was true. It looks like the Psalmist who said: I will remember the deeds of the Lord, I will recall your wonders of old. When you cannot feel the present voice of God, you return to the record of His past faithfulness. You rehearse what He has done. You remind yourself — not as a performance, but as a discipline — of who He has shown Himself to be. You do not manufacture a feeling. You make a decision about where to anchor your trust.

## The Other Side of Silence

Every season of silence in Scripture has an end. It may not end when you want it to, or in the way you expect, or with the answer you were hoping for. But it ends. The four hundred years of prophetic silence between Malachi and Matthew ended with an angel speaking to a priest in a temple, and then to a young woman in Nazareth. The silence between Good Friday and Easter Sunday — the most significant silence in all of history — ended on the third day.

If you are in the silence right now, I want to say this to you directly: it will end. God has not forgotten you. He has not abandoned the purpose He placed in you. He is not far. He is doing something in the silence that the noise would have prevented — something deep, something real, something that will sustain you through everything that is coming.

Hold on. Keep praying. Keep the windows open toward Jerusalem. The morning is coming.

## A Prayer for the Silent Season

Father, I bring You the silence today — the season where Your voice has felt distant and Your presence has felt thin. I do not come with accusation. I come with honesty. I miss You. I want to hear You. I want to feel the nearness that I have experienced in other seasons.

But even in this silence, I choose to trust what I know about You rather than what I feel right now. I know that You are present. I know that Your compassions never fail. I know that the silence is not absence, and that You are doing something in it that I cannot yet see.

Keep me faithful in the waiting. Keep me attentive. Keep the windows open. And when You speak again — in whatever way You choose — let me be the kind of person who recognises Your voice, because the silence made me listen more carefully.

In Jesus' name, Amen.
