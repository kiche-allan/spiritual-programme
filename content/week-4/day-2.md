## Revelation

There is a critical distinction in 2 Corinthians 7:10 that every believer needs to understand. Worldly sorrow says: I feel terrible about what I did. Godly sorrow says: I understand what my sin cost, and I am turning away from it completely. One is a feeling. The other is a decision. Many people confuse emotional regret with genuine repentance — and they wonder why they keep returning to the same sins. The emotion passes, and so does the resolve.

The Greek word for repentance is metanoia — literally, a change of mind. But in the Hebrew tradition it carried even richer meaning: shub, to turn, to return. Repentance is not primarily about how you feel about the past. It is about the direction you face going forward. You were walking one way; you stop, turn completely, and walk the other way. The old direction loses its pull not because you are stronger, but because your mind has genuinely changed about where it leads.

The prodigal son in Luke 15 gives us the most human portrait of repentance in Scripture. Notice the sequence: he came to his senses, he articulated the truth about his situation, he made a decision, and then — crucially — he got up and went. Repentance that does not move is not yet complete. And when he arrived, his father ran to meet him. This is what waits for you on the other side of genuine repentance: not reluctant acceptance, but a Father running toward you while you are still a long way off.

## Morning Prayer

Father, I come to You today not only with words of sorrow but with a turned heart. I do not want to be someone who feels bad and continues unchanged. I want the genuine metanoia — the complete change of mind and direction that Your Word describes.

Where I have been walking in the wrong direction — in patterns of thought, habit, relationship, or choice — I stop today. I turn. I face You. And I receive the promise of Acts 3:19: times of refreshing from the Lord. Repentance is not a punishment — it is the doorway to refreshment. I walk through it today.

Like the prodigal son, I have come to my senses. I have articulated the truth about where I have been. And now I get up and go to You. Run to me, Father. I am still a long way off — but I am coming.

## Daily Practices

- Read Luke 15:11–32 in full — the full parable. Identify where you are in the story right now. Are you still in the far country? On the road home? Already at the feast but struggling with the older brother's attitude?
- Journal: "What is the difference between how I have felt sorry in the past and what genuine repentance — a real change of direction — would look like in my specific situation today?"
- Identify one pattern or habit you have been confessing repeatedly without turning from. Ask God specifically for the grace of metanoia — not just sorrow, but a changed mind about where that path leads.
- Read Ezekiel 36:26 — "I will give you a new heart." God does not just forgive the old heart; He replaces it. Receive this promise. Sit with it for five minutes in gratitude.
