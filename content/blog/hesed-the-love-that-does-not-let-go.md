There is a word in the Hebrew Bible that English cannot fully translate. Translators have tried — they rotate through kindness, mercy, steadfast love, loyal love, and loving-kindness — and every version captures something true while missing something essential. The word is hesed. And it appears over 250 times across the Old Testament, from Genesis to Malachi, as if the writers kept reaching for it because no other word would do.

Hesed is not the love that stays because it is easy. It is not the love that gives because it has surplus. It is not the love that shows up when the conditions are favourable and quietly withdraws when they are not. Hesed is the love that persists beyond what is required. It is the love that has every reason to leave and chooses to stay. It is covenant faithfulness pressed into the texture of daily, ordinary, costly choices — and it is the single word the Bible uses most consistently to describe the character of God.

## Ruth: The Human Face of Hesed

The book of Ruth is the most concentrated study of hesed in the entire Bible. In four short chapters, the word appears three times — and each time it names something that should not, by ordinary logic, be happening.

The story opens in catastrophe. Naomi has lost her husband and both her sons in a foreign country. She is returning to Bethlehem with nothing — no husband, no sons, no future, no prospects. She is, by every measure of the ancient world, finished. She tells her daughters-in-law to go back to their own families, to find new husbands, to build new lives. Orpah kisses her goodbye and leaves. Reasonably. Logically. There is nothing left to stay for.

Ruth stays.

Her words to Naomi have echoed across three thousand years because they describe something that does not come from calculation or obligation. Where you go I will go. Where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God my God. This is hesed. Ruth had been released from every obligation to Naomi. There was nothing left that required her presence. She stayed anyway — not because she had to, but because she was the kind of person whose love does not run after easier options.

When Boaz notices Ruth gleaning in his field and asks about her, his foreman says: she is the Moabite who came back with Naomi. And Boaz, without knowing her, already knows enough. He has heard what she did. He leaves extra grain in her path deliberately. He tells his workers to protect her. He gives her water from his own supply. And when Naomi hears who Ruth has been working for, she says the word: he has not stopped showing his hesed — his loving-kindness — to the living and the dead.

Later, Boaz says it about Ruth herself. This hesed you are showing is greater than the first. You have not run after younger men, whether rich or poor. The word moves like a river through the story — from Ruth to Naomi, from Boaz to Ruth, from God to both of them — because hesed, when it is present in a life, produces more hesed. It is contagious in the best possible way. One act of faithfulness creates the conditions for another.

But the book of Ruth is not ultimately a love story between two people. It is a love story between God and His people, told through the ordinary choices of a Moabite widow and a Bethlehem farmer. God's name never appears directly in the book. There are no miracles, no angels, no dramatic divine interventions. And yet every frame of the story carries the fingerprints of a God who is quietly, invisibly, sovereignly working through the faithful ordinary choices of people who love Him and love others. The hesed Ruth shows to Naomi is a reflection of the hesed God shows to both of them. The coincidence of the field, the timing of Boaz's arrival, the ancient laws of gleaning and redemption that provide exactly the mechanism needed — none of it is random. All of it is hesed.

## The God Who Will Not Let Go

The book of Ruth is a beautiful illustration of hesed. But it is not the primary source. The primary source is God Himself.

Moses asks to see God's glory on the mountain, and God responds by proclaiming His name. The proclamation in Exodus 34:6–7 is considered the most important self-disclosure of God in the entire Old Testament. It is the text that is quoted and alluded to more times than any other in the Hebrew Bible. And at the centre of it is hesed: the Lord, the Lord, the compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in hesed and faithfulness, maintaining hesed to thousands, and forgiving wickedness, rebellion and sin.

Abounding in hesed. Maintaining hesed to thousands. This is not the language of reluctant compliance or minimal obligation. This is a God who overflows in covenant faithfulness, whose hesed is not a small reserve carefully rationed but an inexhaustible supply that presses outward toward the very people who have done the least to deserve it.

Psalm 136 repeats the same phrase twenty-six times — once per verse, like a liturgical drumbeat: His hesed endures forever. His hesed endures forever. His hesed endures forever. The Psalmist goes through the entire history of God's dealings with Israel — creation, the exodus, the wilderness, the conquest, the settled land — and at every point, the refrain is the same. His hesed endures forever. Not his power alone, not his sovereignty alone, but his covenant faithfulness, his loyal love, his refusal to let go — that endures. Forever.

Lamentations 3 is perhaps the most striking witness to this truth, because it comes from the deepest possible darkness. Jerusalem has been destroyed. The temple is rubble. The people are in exile. The writer has been through things that would justify despair and bitterness in any human assessment. And from inside that devastation, he writes what has become one of the most quoted passages in all of Scripture: because of the Lord's great hesed we are not consumed, for his compassions never fail. They are new every morning. Great is your faithfulness.

Written in ruins. That is where this testimony comes from. Not from a comfortable seat in a prosperous season, but from the wreckage of everything — and still: his hesed endures. Still: new every morning. The hesed of God is not a fair-weather quality. It is precisely in the ruins that it becomes most visible, because it is in the ruins that human love has already exhausted itself and withdrawn.

## The Cross: Where Hesed Became Flesh

Everything the Old Testament says about hesed is preparation. Every act of Ruth's loyalty, every field Boaz left extra grain in, every repetition of the enduring love in Psalm 136, every dawn of new mercies in Lamentations — all of it was pointing forward to the moment when the God who is hesed would walk into the world He made and demonstrate what that word actually costs.

The incarnation itself is an act of hesed. The eternal Son of God, before whom the angels bow, the One through whom all things were made — He did not consider equality with God something to be grasped. He emptied Himself. He took on flesh, limitations, hunger, thirst, tiredness, grief. He submitted to the poverty of a manger and the indignity of a borrowed tomb. He came to a people who were not looking for Him, who would reject Him, who would hand Him over to be crucified. He came anyway. He had every reason — by any ordinary logic of love — to stay where He was. He came anyway. That is hesed.

But it is at the cross where the word reaches its fullest, most shattering meaning.

Consider what was happening when Christ was crucified. Not the theology alone — the human reality of it. He had been betrayed by one of His closest friends. He had been abandoned by the others. He had been falsely accused, mocked, beaten, stripped, and nailed to wood. He had the power to end it at any moment — twelve legions of angels available at a word. He had every possible reason, in every possible sense, to leave.

He did not leave.

He stayed. Through the darkness of the sixth hour, through the cry of dereliction — My God, my God, why have you forsaken me — through the thirst, the slow suffocation, the weight of every sin ever committed by every human being who had ever lived or would ever live. He stayed. Not because He had no choice. Because He had made a choice, before time began, to love people who would crucify Him. And that choice was not reversed by the nails.

This is hesed at its absolute fullest. Every human expression of loyal love in the Bible — Ruth staying with Naomi, Boaz leaving grain in the field, Hosea taking back his unfaithful wife, God maintaining His covenant through Israel's generations of faithlessness — all of it is a shadow of this moment. The shadow was real. But the substance is the cross.

Romans 5:8 says it simply: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. Not after we improved. Not after we showed some sign of returning the love. While we were still enemies — Colossians 1:21 uses that word, enemies — He died for us. This is the logic of hesed taken to its ultimate conclusion. It does not wait for the beloved to become worthy. It acts from the character of the lover. God loved us not because we were loveable but because He is love. The hesed was in Him before it was ever directed at us.

## The Resurrection: Hesed Vindicated

But hesed does not end at the cross. If it did, it would be a beautiful tragedy — the most magnificent love story ever told, ending in death.

The resurrection is the declaration that hesed is not defeated by death. It is the announcement that the love that would not let go — not of humanity at the garden, not of Israel in the wilderness, not of Ruth in her poverty, not of us in our sin — has now proven that there is nothing, not even death itself, that can make it stop.

The empty tomb is the ultimate expression of a love that endures forever. The hesed that Psalm 136 celebrated in the exodus, that Lamentations held onto in the ruins, that Ruth demonstrated in the field, that Boaz enacted in the gleaning laws — it has now been demonstrated to be indestructible. He is not here. He is risen. The love that had every reason to give up did not give up. And now it never will.

Paul reaches for the fullest possible language in Romans 8:38–39 and still feels like he is falling short: neither death nor life, neither angels nor demons, neither the present nor the future, nor any powers, neither height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God that is in Christ Jesus our Lord. The hesed is permanent. It is sealed by the resurrection. It cannot be revoked.

## What This Means for You

The hesed of God is not a theological concept to be admired from a distance. It is a present-tense reality that presses into your specific situation today.

Whatever you have done — whatever consistency of failure, whatever repeated return to the same sin, whatever distance you have placed between yourself and God — the hesed has not run out. Lamentations 3:22 was written from ruins. The new mercies arrived the next morning anyway. They arrived the morning after that. They are arriving this morning.

And the hesed of God is not only something to receive. It is something to become. 1 John 4:11 says: dear friends, since God so loved us, we also ought to love one another. Micah 6:8 says that what God requires of you is to love hesed — to love mercy, to love loyal faithfulness, to become the kind of person whose love does not run after easier options.

Ruth became the great-grandmother of David. David became the ancestor of Jesus. The hesed Ruth showed to one destitute widow in a field in Moab rippled forward through generations and became part of the lineage of the One whose hesed would be demonstrated on a cross outside Jerusalem a thousand years later. Your hesed matters. The loyalty you show in small places, the faithfulness you maintain when no one is watching, the love you refuse to withdraw from a person who has given you every reason to — it is not inconsequential. It is the substance through which the character of God becomes visible in the world.

The love does not let go. It never has. It never will.

Come back to it today. Come back as often as you need to. The hesed is new every morning.
