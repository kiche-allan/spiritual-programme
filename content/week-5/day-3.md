## Revelation

Meekness is one of the most misunderstood words in the Christian vocabulary. It is consistently confused with weakness — with passivity, with the absence of will or strength. But the Greek word praus was used in the ancient world to describe a war horse that had been trained — an animal of enormous power that had learned to place that power under the rider's control. Meekness is not the absence of strength. It is strength under perfect submission.

Christ on the cross is the supreme definition of meekness. He who spoke galaxies into existence, He before whom every angel bowed — He allowed Himself to be nailed to wood. Not because He lacked the power to stop it. Because He chose submission to the Father's will over the exercise of His own. The cross is the most powerful act in all of history, and it was accomplished through the most complete meekness ever displayed. This is the model Colossians calls us to — not the meekness of the weak, but the meekness of those who have been so filled with the love of God that they no longer need to assert themselves at the expense of others.

Mercy flows from having received it. Ephesians 2 says God is rich in mercy — not reluctant, not transactional, but abundantly, lavishly merciful. And the person who has truly experienced that mercy — who has stood at the cross and understood the depth of what was extended to them — cannot help but extend it to others. The forgiveness in Colossians 3:13 — forgive as the Lord forgave you — is not a suggestion. It is the natural outflow of a life shaped by the cross. You were shown mercy while you were an enemy. What will you show to the people who have hurt you?

## Morning Prayer

Lord Jesus, teach me Your meekness. Not the meekness of someone without will, but the meekness of someone whose will has been submitted so completely to the Father that they no longer need to fight for themselves. You stood before Pilate with the power to end the world — and You said nothing. That silence was not weakness. It was the most powerful thing anyone has ever done.

And teach me the mercy that flows from the cross. I confess that I find mercy easy in the abstract and difficult in the particular — especially toward those who have wounded me most. But when I stand at the cross and receive what was given to me while I was still Your enemy, how can I withhold it from anyone? Let the depth of what I have received be the measure of what I give.

Clothe me today — with compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, patience. Not as a performance of Christian virtue, but as the natural garments of a person who has been so loved by God that love has become their reflex.

## Daily Practices

- Read Numbers 12:3 — "Moses was a very humble man, more humble than anyone else on the face of the earth." Then read Numbers 12:1–8 for context. Moses held enormous authority. Reflect on the relationship between meekness and true, lasting influence.
- Identify one person toward whom you have been withholding mercy — consciously or not. Pray for them specifically today. Ask God to give you the capacity to extend what you yourself have received.
- Read the parable of the unmerciful servant in Matthew 18:21–35. Let the contrast between the debt cancelled and the debt withheld be a mirror for your own life. Ask: which servant am I in this story right now?
- Journal: "What would genuine meekness look like in the most difficult relationship in my life right now? What strength would I need to put under submission — and what would be the fruit of doing so?"
