## Revelation

The year is 701 BC. Sennacherib, king of Assyria, has already swallowed every nation in his path. His army is the most feared military force the ancient world has ever seen. And now the letter arrives — a piece of correspondence designed not merely to threaten but to psychologically destroy. It lists every nation that has already fallen. It asks the question with cold logic: what makes you think your God is any different from the gods of the nations I have already destroyed? It is the most sophisticated crisis Hezekiah has ever faced, and there is no military answer, no political solution, no human path through it.

What Hezekiah does next is one of the most instructive moments in the entire Old Testament. He does not call a war council. He does not draft a diplomatic response. He does not collapse in despair. He takes the letter — this physical object that represents the full weight of his crisis — and he walks up to the temple, and he spreads it out before God. That act is worth sitting with for a long time. He did not summarise the problem. He did not give God a bullet-pointed briefing. He unfolded the letter and laid it down, as if to say: You see this. You read it. Now respond. There is a profound theological confidence in that act. Hezekiah believed that God could read his mail. He believed that the details of his crisis were not beneath God's attention. He believed that the same God who made heaven and earth was interested in one king's specific impossible situation.

This is the invitation the crisis is bringing to you today. Not to pretend the letter does not exist. Not to spiritualise it away. But to take the actual, specific, documented weight of what you are facing — and spread it out before God. The crisis is not evidence that God is absent. It is the very circumstance that reveals whether your trust is real. Hezekiah's trust was not a pleasant spiritual feeling he had during calm seasons. It was a decision he made when the walls were closing in. That is the only kind of trust that means anything.

## Morning Prayer

Lord God, I come to You this morning the way Hezekiah came — with the letter in my hand. The specific thing that is pressing on me, the situation that has no visible solution, the fear that surfaces in the night when I cannot distract myself from it. I do not come with a tidy summary. I spread it out before You. You see it. You know every detail. And I trust that the God who made heaven and earth is not overwhelmed by what is overwhelming me.

I confess that my first instinct in crisis is often not prayer. It is strategy, or worry, or the desperate search for a human solution. Forgive me for treating prayer as a last resort when it is the most powerful first move available. I choose today to begin where Hezekiah began — in the temple, before You, with the impossible thing fully acknowledged and fully submitted.

You are God over all the kingdoms of the earth. You are God over this situation. I trust You not because the outcome is already clear but because You are already here — present, attentive, and more than sufficient for everything I am carrying.

## Daily Practices

- Read Isaiah 37:1–20 in full — the complete account of Hezekiah's crisis and prayer. Note every specific detail of what he did before he prayed, how he prayed, and what he asked God to do. Let his posture become a model for your own.
- Take the specific crisis or pressure you are currently carrying and write it down in full — every detail, as if writing the letter Hezekiah received. Then physically place your hand on the paper and pray over it. Let the act of spreading it before God be as deliberate as Hezekiah's was.
- Journal: "What is my instinctive first response when crisis arrives — strategy, worry, or prayer? What would it look like for prayer to become genuinely first, not last?" Be honest rather than aspirational.
- Read Psalm 46 in full as your declaration for today. Note especially verse 10 — "Be still and know that I am God." In the middle of the crisis, stillness is not passivity. It is the most radical act of trust available to you.
